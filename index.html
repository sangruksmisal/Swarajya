Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj

1630 – 1680

Founder of the Maratha Empire • Builder of Swarajya

✦

In an age when the Mughal Empire stretched across almost all of the Indian subcontinent, when the great Deccan Sultanates were either collapsing or capitulating, and when no Indian ruler had successfully challenged the combined military and administrative weight of Mughal power — a boy was born in a hilltop fort in the Sahyadri mountains.

That boy grew up to build something no one had built in the Deccan for generations: a kingdom of, by, and for the people of the land. He called it Hindavi Swarajya — self-rule in the language of the people. He was twenty-seven years old when the idea became a reality. He was forty-nine when he died. In those twenty-two years, he changed the course of Indian history.

✉

Born at Shivneri

February 19, 1630. His mother Jijabai had climbed to Shivneri fort for safety while his father Shahaji Raje was on campaign. She prayed at the Shivai Devi temple for a brave son. She named him Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj — after the goddess who answered her prayer.

⚔

First Fort at Sixteen

In 1646, at the age of sixteen, Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj captured Torna Fort — his first fort. He did not ask permission from his father, the Deccan Sultanates, or the Mughals. He simply took it. Within a decade, he held a string of forts across the Sahyadri mountains.

♔

Master of Guerrilla Warfare

Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj invented a style of warfare suited to the Sahyadri terrain — fast cavalry raids, night attacks, and fort-to-fort strategy that made the mountains themselves his weapon. Mughal generals with armies ten times the size could not pin him down. He called it ganimi kava — the enemy's own tactics, turned against them.

⚓

India's First Navy

Recognising that whoever controls the sea controls the trade, Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj built India's first organised navy. He constructed Sindhudurg and Vijaydurg, challenged the Portuguese and English at sea, and seized Khanderi at the door of Bombay harbour. No Indian ruler before him had thought this way about the ocean.

☯

A Just Administration

Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj banned looting of civilians in war — an extraordinary rule in the 17th century. He protected temples and mosques alike. He appointed men of talent regardless of caste or religion. His navy included Muslim commanders; his personal bodyguard included men from every community. Swarajya was for the people of the land — all of them.

♚

Crowned Chhatrapati

On June 6, 1674, at Raigad fort, Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj was crowned Chhatrapati — Sovereign of the Realm. It was the first Hindu coronation in the Deccan in centuries. His mother Jijabai, old and frail, came to watch. She died twelve days later, having seen the fulfilment of everything she had prayed for.

The Forts

Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj built, captured, or strengthened over 360 forts across the Sahyadri mountains, the Konkan coast, and the Deccan plateau. Each fort was not merely a military installation — it was an administrative centre, a storehouse, a refuge for the people of the surrounding villages, and a symbol of the idea that this land had rulers who were answerable to its people.

The forts were his infrastructure. They were what made Swarajya durable — not dependent on any single battle or any single general, but distributed across hundreds of strongpoints that each had to be taken individually. Aurangzeb spent 27 years in the Deccan trying. He could not finish the job.

His Legacy and Impact on India

When Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj died in 1680, Aurangzeb believed Swarajya would die with him. It did not. The Maratha Empire that grew from the seed Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj planted would, within a century, become the largest power in India — controlling territory from the Khyber Pass in the northwest to Thanjavur in the south, from Orissa in the east to the Arabian Sea in the west.

The Marathas were the power that finally ended Mughal dominance of the subcontinent. By the mid-18th century, the Mughal Emperor in Delhi was effectively a pensioner of the Maratha Confederacy. The British East India Company, in its final push for Indian dominance, identified the Marathas — not the Mughals — as its primary adversary. Three Anglo-Maratha Wars were fought. The British won the third, in 1818. It took them 138 years after Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj's death to finish what Aurangzeb could not.

Beyond politics and territory, Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj's legacy is cultural and spiritual. He demonstrated that a son of the Deccan soil — not a foreign dynasty, not a distant empire — could protect, govern, and inspire the people of India. That idea outlasted every empire that tried to erase it. It lives today in every fort on this map.

"Do not think that this is a small thing I have done. Hindavi Swarajya — self-rule — is the will of God. God is making me the instrument of his will." — Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj
